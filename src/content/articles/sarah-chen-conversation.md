Sarah Chen's work exists at the intersection of material research, meditative practice, and conceptual rigor. Working primarily with handmade paper, she creates delicate sculptural forms and wall-based works that explore the relationship between light, shadow, and the passage of time.

## The Beginning

**Julia Wong:** Your work with paper has a deeply meditative quality. Can you talk about how you came to this material?

**Sarah Chen:** I came to paper almost by accident. During my residency in Japan, I encountered washi-making for the first time. The process—the cooking of fibers, the beating, the sheet formation—struck me immediately as a form of meditation. Each sheet is a record of a specific moment, specific conditions of light, temperature, humidity. No two sheets are ever exactly the same.

That realization changed my entire approach to art. I became interested not in the final object but in the process—repetitive, slow, attentive. The work became a practice rather than a production.

## Light and Absence

**JW:** Your recent works seem to be as much about absence as presence—the shadows they cast are as important as the paper itself.

**SC:** That's very perceptive. I think of my work as creating conditions for light to manifest. The paper is a membrane between light and shadow. When I fold or perforate the surface, I'm creating passages—for light, for air, for the viewer's gaze. The work isn't complete until light enters the space.

In a way, I see this as a continuation of traditional East Asian aesthetics—the appreciation of empty space, of what is not shown. But it's also very contemporary. We live in an age of information overload, constant stimulation. There's a deep need for spaces of quiet, for works that invite slowing down.

## Memory and Material

**JW:** You often speak about memory in relation to your work. How does the material of paper carry memory?

**SC:** Paper is literally made from plant fibers—from living material. In that sense, it carries the memory of growth, of seasons, of the natural world. But it also carries the memory of the making process—the beating, the forming, the drying. Each sheet records the specific conditions of its creation.

When I work with older papers, there's an additional layer—the marks of time, of handling, of environmental exposure. These traces become part of the work, adding layers of meaning that I can control but never fully predict.

I think this is why paper resonates so deeply with people. It's a material that everyone has an intimate relationship with—letters, books, photographs, currency. It carries cultural memory as well as material memory. When someone encounters a handmade paper work, they bring all of these associations with them.
