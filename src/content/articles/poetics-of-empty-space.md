In contemporary sculptural practice, the void has emerged as a material in its own right. Where traditional sculpture concerned itself with mass, volume, and the occupation of space, artists today increasingly explore what happens in the spaces between—the negative forms that give shape to our perception of presence.

## The Language of Absence

The concept of negative space in sculpture is not new—Henry Moore's reclining figures famously incorporated holes and voids as essential compositional elements. However, contemporary artists have pushed this investigation further, asking not just how absence shapes form, but how it shapes meaning, experience, and our understanding of materiality itself.

Consider the work of Rachel Whiteread, whose casts of architectural spaces transform the invisible—the air inside a room, the space beneath a chair—into solid form. Or Anish Kapoor's voids, which seem to absorb light and space itself, creating portals to impossible depths. These works remind us that sculpture has always been as much about the space it displaces as the material it occupies.

> "The space within becomes a sculpture. We are not just making objects; we are shaping the air around us."
> — Anish Kapoor

## Silence as Material

In our current exhibition "Between Silence and Form," we see three distinct approaches to this investigation. Liu Wei's architectural interventions create spaces where interior and exterior, solid and void, become indistinguishable. His work asks us to reconsider our assumptions about what constitutes a wall, a room, a boundary.

Sarah Chen's paper works operate on a more intimate scale, but address similar questions. Her folded and perforated surfaces create shadow plays where the work exists as much in its cast shadows as in its physical form. The paper becomes a mediator between light and darkness, presence and absence.

Tomás Saraceno's suspended installations perhaps most directly engage with the poetics of empty space. His web-like structures seem to make visible the invisible forces that shape our environment—gravity, air currents, the delicate balance of ecosystems. The work exists in a state of perpetual becoming, responsive to its environment and viewers.

## Toward a New Materiality

What unites these diverse practices is a shared understanding that sculpture in the 21st century must grapple with immateriality. In an age of digital dematerialization, when so much of our experience occurs in virtual spaces, the question of what constitutes physical presence has become increasingly urgent.

These artists remind us that the physical world is richer and stranger than we often acknowledge. By attending to emptiness, silence, and absence, they reveal the hidden dimensions of our material reality—and invite us to see the world anew.
